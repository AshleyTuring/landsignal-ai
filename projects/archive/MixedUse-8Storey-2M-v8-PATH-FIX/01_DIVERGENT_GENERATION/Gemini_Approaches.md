```
2. Roof type: is Flat
3. Ownership type: is Unbroken Freehold
4. Distance to train station (m): is less than 800
5. Use class (current): is any of C3, E
6. Conservation area: is not Yes
7. Title area (acres): is greater than 0.15 AND less than 1.0
8. Building age: is any of 1970-1990, 1990-2000
```

**Rationale:**
This approach directly addresses the £200k capital limit by focusing on airspace development, a highly capital-efficient strategy. It seeks to acquire the right to build on top of existing 3-4 storey buildings, creating new residential units without the cost of land acquisition. The strategy is refined by targeting buildings from an era often associated with flat roofs and robust concrete frames capable of supporting additional floors. Crucially, filtering for "Unbroken Freehold" titles (Pattern 23A) de-risks the legal process by avoiding the need to negotiate with multiple individual leaseholders. *Note: The "Roof type" filter is assumed to exist; if not, this would become a manual review step using satellite imagery.*

**Strengths:**
- **Highly Capital Efficient:** Acquiring airspace rights is significantly cheaper than land, fitting the budget perfectly.
- **Reduced Legal Complexity:** Targeting unbroken freeholds avoids complex and often fatal negotiations with multiple leaseholders.
- **Sustainable Development:** Creates new homes in dense urban locations with excellent transport links, often allowing for car-free schemes.
- **Faster Construction:** Building on an existing structure can be faster and less disruptive than a full demolition and rebuild.

**Limitations:**
- **Structural Risk:** The existing building's frame may not be able to support additional floors. An early structural survey is critical.
- **Filter Availability:** "Roof type" is a critical filter that may not be available, requiring significant manual work to verify.
- **Rights of Light:** Adding height can impact neighbouring properties' right to light, potentially leading to objections or compensation claims.
- **Construction Access:** Physical access to the roof for construction can be challenging and costly.

**Implementation Strategy:**
1.  Run the appropriate search (London or other cities) to generate targets.
2.  Use satellite and street view imagery to perform an initial triage, verifying roof shape, checking for obvious obstructions, and assessing access.
3.  Approach the freehold owner of the most promising sites with a clear proposal to acquire the airspace rights, subject to a structural survey.
4.  Negotiate a price for the rights, keeping the total initial outlay within the £200k budget.
5.  Commission a structural engineer to conduct a feasibility study.
6.  If feasible, proceed with a planning application for additional storeys.
7.  Once consent is granted, the asset ("airspace with planning") can be sold to a developer or a JV can be formed to build out the scheme.
